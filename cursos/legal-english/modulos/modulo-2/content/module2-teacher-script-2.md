### [38:00] DOCUMENT ANALYSIS INTRODUCTION

**[On screen: Actual Clause 36 text displayed]**

**INSTRUCTOR:**

"Time to analyze a real document. This is Clause 36 from the MUR Shipping contract‚Äîthe actual force majeure clause that was litigated. Let's read it carefully.

**[Read clause aloud, pointing to each element]**

'36.1 Subject to the terms of this Clause 36, neither Owners nor Charterers shall be liable to the other for loss, damage, delay or failure in performance caused by a Force Majeure Event as hereinafter defined.'

So this clause protects both parties‚Äîowners AND charterers‚Äîfrom liability. It covers loss, damage, delay, and failure in performance. Broad protection.

'36.3 A Force Majeure Event is an event or state of affairs which meets ALL of the following criteria...'

**[Emphasize 'ALL']**

ALL. Not some. Not most. ALL four criteria must be met.

'(a) It is outside the immediate control of the Party giving the Force Majeure Notice'

Criterion A: Outside control. US sanctions are certainly outside a private company's control.

'(b) It prevents or delays the loading or discharge of the cargo'

Criterion B: Prevents or delays loading or discharge. The sanctions affected payments, which affected MUR's willingness to ship.

'(c) It is caused by one or more of [listed events including sanctions]'

Criterion C: Must be a listed event. Sanctions were specifically listed. Check.

'(d) It cannot be overcome by reasonable endeavours from the Party affected.'

Criterion D. Here's where the case turned. RTI said MUR could overcome the payment problem by accepting euros‚Äîreasonable endeavours. MUR said no, accepting euros isn't required.

Who was right? The Supreme Court said MUR. Reasonable endeavours means efforts to perform the CONTRACT. Not efforts to accept DIFFERENT performance."

### [41:00] EXERCISE 9

**[On screen: Exercise 9 questions]**

**INSTRUCTOR:**

"Complete Exercise 9. Four questions about the clause.

**[Pause indicator: "PAUSE VIDEO ‚Äî Complete Exercise 9 ‚Äî 3 minutes"]**

Pause the video. Three minutes.

**[Beat]**

Answers:

Question 1: Who is protected from liability under this clause?

**Both Owners‚Äîthat's MUR Shipping‚Äîand Charterers‚Äîthat's RTI‚Äîare protected** from liability for loss, damage, delay, or failure in performance caused by a Force Majeure Event. The clause is mutual.

Question 2: What four criteria must be met?

**(a) The event must be outside the immediate control of the party giving notice.**

**(b) The event must prevent or delay the loading or discharge of cargo.**

**(c) The event must be caused by one of the listed events‚Äîincluding sanctions.**

**(d) The event cannot be overcome by reasonable endeavours.**

All four. Missing even one means you can't invoke force majeure.

Question 3: What is the significance of criterion (d) for the MUR Shipping case?

**This was the central issue.** RTI argued that criterion (d) was not satisfied because MUR could have 'overcome' the sanctions problem by accepting euros. The Supreme Court ruled that 'reasonable endeavours' does NOT require accepting non-contractual performance, so criterion (d) WAS satisfied and MUR could rely on force majeure.

Question 4: Does this clause excuse all types of non-performance?

**No.** The clause specifically covers events affecting 'loading or discharge of cargo.' It must be a listed type of event. And all four criteria must be met. It's not a blanket excuse for any non-performance."

### [47:00] LEGAL LATIN REVIEW

**[On screen: Latin terms table]**

**INSTRUCTOR:**

"Before we finish, let's consolidate the Latin terms from this module. You've seen them in context‚Äînow let's make sure you can use them.

**[Terms appear one by one]**

***Pacta sunt servanda*** ‚Äî Agreements must be kept. The central principle of contract law. When MUR said 'the contract says dollars,' they were invoking *pacta sunt servanda*.

***Consensus ad idem*** ‚Äî Meeting of minds. Both parties must agree to the same terms. Essential for contract formation.

***Quid pro quo*** ‚Äî Something for something. The essence of consideration in common law. You give, I give.

***Vis major*** ‚Äî Superior force. The Latin origin of force majeure. An irresistible force beyond human control.

***Bona fide*** ‚Äî In good faith. While less central in English law than Mexican law, still an important concept.

***Ex contractu*** ‚Äî Arising from the contract. Damages *ex contractu* are damages arising from breach of contract, as opposed to *ex delicto*‚Äîfrom tort."

Question 1: How does force majeure work differently in Mexican law‚Äî*caso fortuito*‚Äîcompared to English law?

Think about this: In Mexico, *caso fortuito* and *fuerza mayor* are codified in Article 2111 of the Civil Code. You get protection automatically if the event meets the legal definition. In English law, nothing is automatic. You must write force majeure into your contract. If you don't, you're stuck with frustration doctrine‚Äîwhich is much harder to invoke.

**[Question 2 appears]**

Question 2: If you were RTI's lawyer, what arguments would you make to show MUR should have accepted euros?

RTI's lawyers argued: MUR would suffer no financial loss. The purpose of the payment clause‚Äîgetting MUR the right amount of money‚Äîwould be achieved. Refusing euros was commercially unreasonable. The Court of Appeal agreed! But the Supreme Court said no‚Äîthe contract is the contract.

**[Question 3 appears]**

Question 3: Why is it important for Mexican lawyers working internationally to understand common law contract principles?

Because you WILL work with US and UK contracts. Your clients will sign them. You will negotiate them. You need to know that consideration is required, that force majeure must be explicit, that the written words control. Misunderstanding these principles could be malpractice."
