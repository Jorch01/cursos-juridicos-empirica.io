Four elements. Let me emphasize: in Mexico, we talk about *consentimiento*, *objeto*, and *causa*. Different framework. Common law requires these four specific elements.

**[Continue with text]**

'Acceptance must mirror the offer exactly‚Äîany changes constitute a counter-offer. This is called the mirror image rule.'

The mirror image rule is strict in common law. If I offer to sell you a car for $10,000 and you say 'I accept, but I'll pay $9,500,' that's NOT acceptance. That's a counter-offer. The original offer dies.

**[Highlight consideration section]**

'Consideration is uniquely important in common law. Each party must provide something of value: money, goods, services, or even a promise to do‚Äîor not do‚Äîsomething.'

Here's where Mexican lawyers get confused. In Mexico, you can have a valid contract based on a promise. In common law? A promise without consideration is a gift. Unenforceable. You need the *quid pro quo*‚Äîsomething for something."

*Pacta sunt servanda*. PAK-tah soont ser-VAN-dah. Agreements must be kept. The contract says dollars, you get dollars. The contract says delivery in June, delivery is in June. What you wrote is what you must do."

### [17:30] COMPARATIVE TABLE

**[On screen: US/UK/Mexico comparison table]**

**INSTRUCTOR:**

"Look at the comparative table in your worksheet. Let's walk through the key differences.

**[Point to each row]**

**Basic requirements:** US and UK need offer, acceptance, and consideration. Mexico needs *consentimiento*, *objeto*, and *causa*. Similar but not identical concepts.

**Consideration:** Required in US and UK. Not required in Mexico‚Äîwe use *causa* instead. This is the biggest difference. A Mexican lawyer might draft a contract perfectly valid in Mexico that would be unenforceable in New York because there's no consideration.

**Written form:** Common law has the Statute of Frauds requiring writing for certain contracts‚Äîland sales, contracts over a year, and so on. Mexico generally requires more contracts in writing. Know which contracts need formal documentation.
