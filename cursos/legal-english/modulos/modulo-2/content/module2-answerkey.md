## Exercise 4: Comprehension Questions

1. **What are the four essential elements of a valid contract in common law?**
   
   The four essential elements are: (1) offer, (2) acceptance, (3) consideration, and (4) intention to create legal relations.

2. **What is the "mirror image rule" and why is it important?**
   
   The mirror image rule requires that acceptance must match the offer exactly. Any changes to the terms constitute a counter-offer, not an acceptance. This is important because it ensures both parties are agreeing to the same terms (*consensus ad idem*).

3. **How does Mexican law differ from common law regarding consideration?**
   
   Mexican law (civil law system) does not require consideration. Instead, it uses *causa* (cause) as the underlying purpose or reason for the contract. A contract can be valid in Mexico without the exchange of value that common law requires.

4. **Why must force majeure clauses be explicitly included in common law contracts?**
   
   Unlike Mexican law where *caso fortuito* is codified in the Civil Code, common law has no general doctrine of force majeure. If parties want protection from unforeseeable events, they must draft specific clauses into their contracts.

# PART 4: PRACTICAL APPLICATION ANSWERS

## Exercise 9: Identify Elements

1. **Who is protected from liability under this clause?**
   
   Both Owners (MUR Shipping) and Charterers (RTI) are protected from liability for loss, damage, delay, or failure in performance caused by a Force Majeure Event.

2. **What four criteria must be met for an event to qualify as force majeure?**

   a) The event must be outside the immediate control of the party giving notice.
   
   b) The event must prevent or delay the loading or discharge of cargo.
   
   c) The event must be caused by one of the listed events (including sanctions).
   
   d) The event cannot be overcome by reasonable endeavours from the affected party.

3. **What is the significance of criterion (d) for the MUR Shipping case?**
   
   Criterion (d) was the central issue in the case. RTI argued that MUR could have "overcome" the sanctions issue by accepting euros, meaning criterion (d) was not satisfied. The Supreme Court ruled that "reasonable endeavours" does not require accepting non-contractual performance, so criterion (d) WAS satisfied and MUR could rely on force majeure.

4. **Does this clause excuse all types of non-performance? Explain.**
   
   No. The clause only excuses non-performance caused by events meeting ALL four criteria. Additionally, the clause specifically covers "loading or discharge of the cargo" ‚Äî it does not necessarily cover all contractual obligations. The event must also be one of the specified types listed in the clause.

# INSTRUCTOR NOTES

## Common Student Errors

1. **Consideration vs. Causa:** Students often conflate these concepts. Emphasize that consideration requires actual exchange of value, while *causa* is about the purpose or reason.

2. **Force Majeure Confusion:** Mexican students expect force majeure to be implied (as *caso fortuito* is in Mexican law). Stress that common law requires explicit clauses.

3. **Reasonable Endeavours:** Students may think this requires doing "everything possible." Clarify it means what a reasonable businessperson would do, not heroic measures‚Äîand per MUR Shipping, not accepting different performance terms.

4. **Latin Pronunciation:** Practice *pacta sunt servanda* (/Ààp√¶kt…ô s änt s…úÀêrÀàv√¶nd…ô/) and *vis major* (/v…™s Ààme…™d í…ôr/).

## Extension Activities

- Have students draft a force majeure clause for a Mexico-US contract
- Discuss how COVID-19 affected force majeure claims globally
- Compare the MUR Shipping case with Mexican Supreme Court decisions on *caso fortuito*

## Cultural Notes for Mexican Students

- In Mexico, *buena fe* (good faith) permeates all contract law; in common law, it's more limited
- Mexican lawyers should advise clients that common law contracts are interpreted literally‚Äîthe written words control
- When drafting for international parties, explicitly define all force majeure events and remedies
